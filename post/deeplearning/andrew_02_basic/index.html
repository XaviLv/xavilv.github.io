Binary Classification

Logistic regression is an algorithm for binary classification.

In computer, an image is stored by 3 matrices, representing intensily values of the R, G, B channels seperately. And pixels in each channel will be unrolled into a feature vector.

Dimention of the input feature vector:

$$ n = n_x = 64643 = 12288 $$

Notations

In the binary classification, our goal is to learn a classifier that can input an image by this feature vector X.

A single training example will be donated as:

$$(x,y)\quad x\in\reals^{n_x},; y\in{0,1}$$

m training examples:

$${(x^{(1)},y^{(1)}),; (x^{(2)},y^{(2)}),;…;, (x^{(m)},y^{(m)})}$$

where x is the feature vector and y is the label. And the superscript parentheses i is associated with the i-th training example.

Training examples as a more compact notation, stacking them in Columns:

  • feature vector

$$X = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & … & | \cr x^{(1)} & x^{(2)} & … & x^{(m)} \cr | & | & … & | \end{bmatrix} \quad X\in\reals^{n_x*m} $$

  • label vector

$$Y = \begin{bmatrix} y^{(1)}&y^{(2)}&…&y^{(m)} \end{bmatrix} \quad Y\in\reals^{1*m} $$


Logistic Regression Model

Goal

Try to learn parameters W and B so that Y hat becomes a good estimate of the chance of Y being equal to one.
Y hat, the estimate of Y, stands for the probability of the chance that Y is equal to one given the input features x.

$$ Given: \quad x, \quad want \quad \hat{y} = P(y=1|x) ,\quad x\in\reals^{n_x} \newline Parameters: \quad w\in\reals^{n_x}, \quad b\in\reals \newline Output: \quad \hat{y} = \sigma(w^Tx + b) $$

$$ \sigma(z) = \dfrac{1}{1 + e^{-z}} $$


Logistic Regress Cost Function

Loss(Error) Function is defined to measure how good our output y-hat is when the true label is y, which must be a convex function. So that there is only one local optimum (i.e. the global optimum).

$$ \ell(\hat{y},y) = -(ylog\hat{y} + (1-y)log(1-\hat{y})) $$

Intuition why this loss funciton makes sense:

$$ If ;y = 1: \quad \ell(\hat{y},y) = -log\hat{y}, \quad \hat{y}\to1 \newline If ;y = 0: \quad \ell(\hat{y},y) = log(1-\hat{y}), \quad \hat{y}\to0 $$

The loss function is defined with respect to a single training example, measuring how well you’re doing on a single training example.

Cost Function measures how well you do on the entire training set, which is the cost of your parameters.

$$ J(w,b) = {1 \over m}\sum_{i=1}^{m}\ell(\hat{y}^{(i)}, y^{(i)}) = -{{1 \over m}y^{(i)}}\sum{i=1}^{m}[log\hat{y}^{(i)} + (1-y^{(i)})log(1-\hat{y}^{(i)}) $$

We’re going to find the parameters that minimize the overall cost function J.


Gradient Descent

single example

It starts at the initial point and then takes a step in the steepest downhill direction. After enough iterations, it would converge to the global optimum point, or just get close to it.

$$ w := w - \alpha{\partial J(w,b) \over \partial w} \newline b := b - \alpha{\partial J(w,b) \over \partial b} $$

where alpha means the learning rate and in code we just use dw to donate the partial derivative, representing how much the cost function slopes in the w direction:

$$ \partial J(w) \over \partial w $$

therefore the iteration is coded as follows,

1
2

w = w - alpha * dw
b = bs - alpha * db

If

$$ z \rightleftarrows a = \sigma(z) \rightleftarrows \ell(a,y) $$

then

$$ {\partial{\ell(a,y)} \over \partial{a}} = {\partial{(-yloga - (1-y)log(1-a))} \over \partial{a}} = {-y \over a} + {1-y \over 1-a} \newline {\partial{\ell(a,y)} \over \partial{z}} = {\partial{\ell(a,y)} \over {\partial{a}}} * {da \over dz} = a - y $$

Let me explain how this worked out.

Suppose

$$ f(z) = {1 \over a} = 1 + e^{-z} \newline $$

then

$$ \tag{1} f’(z) = ({1 \over a})’ = - {a’ \over a^2} $$

$$ \tag{2} f’(z) = (1 + e^{-z})’ = (e^{-z})’ = -e^{-z} = 1 - f(z) = 1 - {1 \over a} $$

so

$$ {a’ \over a^2} = {1 - a \over a} \implies a’ = a(1-a) \newline where \quad a’ = {da \over dz} $$

therefore

$$ {\partial{\ell(a,y)} \over \partial(z)} = ({-y \over a} + {1-y \over 1-a}) * a(1-a) = a - y $$

Done!

Vectorization

Vectorization is the art of getting rid of the explicit for-loops in your code.

E.g.

Both GPU & CPU have the parallelization instructions, also known as SIMD (i.e. single instruction multi-data) instructions. If you use the built-in functions such as np.function (e.g. np.dot, np.exp, np.log, np.abs, np.maximum, .etc), it enables Python numpy to take much better advantage of parallelism, to make your computation much faster.

Logistic Regression Explanation

Since

$$ p(y=1|x) = \hat{y} $$

then

$$ If ;y=1: \quad p(y|x) = \hat{y} \newline If ;y=0: \quad p(y|x) = 1 - \hat{y} $$

so

$$ p(y|x) = \hat{y}^{y}(1-\hat{y})^{(1-y)} $$

Because the log function is a strictly monotonically increasing function, you’re maximizing log(p(y|x)) give you similar result that is optimizing p(y|x).

Therefore

$$ logP(y|x) = ylog\hat{y} + (1-y)log(1-\hat{y}) = -\ell(\hat{y}, y) $$

As shown above, to minimize the loss value equals maximizing the probability of P(y|x).

Assume the training examples are independent and identically distributed (IID, 独立同分布).

$$ logP(Y|X) = log\prod_{i=1}^{m}P(y^{(i)}|x^{(i)}) = \sum_{i=1}^{m} $$

In statistics, there’s a principle called Maximum Likelihood Estimation, which just means to choose the parameters that maximize things like logP(Y|X) here. In other words, our goal is to minimize the cost.
