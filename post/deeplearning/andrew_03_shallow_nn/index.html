Every neuron corresponds to two steps of calculation: one is to compute z (linear expression), and the other to compute a (non-linear expression).

We’ll use a superscript square bracket to refer to quantities associated with this stack of nodes called a layer.

$$ z^{[1]} = w^{[1]}x + b^{[1]} $$

Neural Network Representation

As demonstrated in the image below, when talking about the count of layers in a neural network, the input layer is not involved.

Each circle(node) in a layer represents two steps of computation.

  • Linear: compute z
  • Non-linear: compute the activation as the sigmoid function of z

So, as in this example, we can do the following calculations to carry out the whole process.

$$ z_1^{[1]} = w_1^{[1]T}x + b_1^{[1]} \quad a_1^{[1]} = \sigma(z_1^{[1]}) \newline z_2^{[1]} = w_2^{[1]T}x + b_2^{[1]} \quad a_2^{[1]} = \sigma(z_2^{[1]}) \newline … $$

In order to accelerate the calculation, we should take advantage of the vectorization technique, instead of manually looping.

$$ Z^{[1]} = W^{[1]T}X + b^{[1]} \newline a^{[1]} = \sigma(Z^{[1]}) $$

Expanded by stacking them in rows:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} z_1^{[1]} \cr z_2^{[1]} \cr z_3^{[1]} \cr z_4^{[1]} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} —— w_1^{[1]T} —— \cr —— w_2^{[1]T} —— \cr —— w_3^{[1]T} —— \cr —— w_4^{[1]T} —— \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \cr x_2 \cr x_3 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} b_1^{[1]} \cr b_2^{[1]} \cr b_3^{[1]} \cr b_4^{[1]} \end{bmatrix} $$

where, $$ w^{[1]T}.shape = (4,3) $$

Note: The sigmoid function should also be implemented in a vectorized way to maximize the functionality of matrix.


Vectorizing across multiple examples

We define the matrix X to be equal to our m training examples stacked up in these columns like below. So the horizontal index corresponds to different training examples. As you sweep through from left to right, you’re scanning through the training set. And vertically, the vertical index corresponds to different nodes (i.e. hidden units) in the neural network.

$$ X = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & & | \cr x^{(1)} & x^{(2)} & … & x^{(m)} \cr | & | & & | \end{bmatrix} $$

where, $$ X.shape = (n_x, m)$$

And the n-subscript-x means the dimension of each sample feature vector.

Similarly,

$$ A^{[1]} = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & & | \cr a^{1} & a^{1} & … & a^{1} \cr | & | & & | \end{bmatrix} $$

So,

$$ Z^{[1]} = W^{[1]T}X + b^{[1]} $$


Activation Functions


**Tanh & Sigmoid**

tanh almost always works better* than sigmoid as an activation function. Because with values between +1 and -1, the mean of the activations that come out of your head is closer to having a zero mean.

Sometimes when you training the algorithm, you might center the data and have your data have zero mean. Using tanh instead of sigmoid kind of having the effect of centering your data, which actually makes learning for the next layer much easier.

One exception is that when you’re using binary classification, in which case you might use the sigmoid activation function for the output layer.

**Downside:** If z is either very large or very small, the gradient of the derivative or the slope of this function becomes very small, close to zero. So this would slow down the gradient descent.


**ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit)**

The slope or derivative is 1 when z is positive and 0 when it’s not.


**Rules of Thumb**

If your output is 0 or 1, meaning you’re doing the binary classification, the sigmoid function is very natural for the output layer. And then for all the hidden units ReLU is increasingly the default choice of the activation function.

**Downside:** The derivative is equal to zero when z is negative. There is another version called Leaky ReLU, which is usually better than ReLU although not used as much in practice.

For half of the range of z, the slope of ReLU is zero, but in practice enough of your hidden units will have z greater than zero. So learning can still be quite fast for most training examples.


Gradient Descent for NN (backpropagation)

When we talk about the logistic regression, we have this ,forward pass where we compute z, then a, then the loss. And then to take the derivatives, we have this backward pass where we could first compute da, then go on to dz, and then dw, db.

The definition for the loss was,

$$ \ell(a, y) = -y\log{a} - (1-y)\log{(1-a)} $$

And if we figure out this calculus, then,

$$ {d \over da}\ell(a, y) = -{y \over a} + {1-y \over 1-a} $$

Since the derivative of log(x) is 1/x.

Note: When programming in code, we usually use the da to represent the derivative of the loss function over a.

And according to the chamber of calculus,

$$ {\partial(a, y) \over \partial{z}} = {\partial(a, y) \over \partial{a}} * {da \over dz} $$


Random Initialization

When training a neural network, it is important to initialize the parameters randomly. Otherwise, no matter how long you train the network, all hidden units are still computing the same function all the time. So in this case, there is really no point of having more than one hidden units.

Take this network above as an example,

$$ w^{[1]} = np.random.randn(2,2) * 0.01 \newline b^{[1]} = np.zeros(2) \newline w^{[2]} = np.random.randn(1,2) * 0.01 \newline b^{[2]} = 0 $$

Usually, the weights will be multiplied by a very smalll number to be initialized into very small random values. So that if you’re using sigmoid or tanh activation functions inside the network, they won’t be saturated thus slowing down the learning.
